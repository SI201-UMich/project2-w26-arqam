Title: Reflection

1. After web scraping data off of Airbnb as well as analyzing my resulting dataset,
   I believe the current system doesn't provide accountability and is inadequate.
   Most of the listings within my CSV dataset, that was the result of getting data 
   directly from the Airbnb website, seem to have some policy numbers that don't 
   validate the Engineering Code of Ethics. Particularly, Principle 3 says that engineers
   should ensure their products meet the highest professional standards possible and 
   use accurate data derived by ethical means (3.13, 3.14). If Airbnb's system displays
   missing or unverified policy numbers, it suggests that the platform may not be aligning
   with principle 3 of the Engineering Code of Ethics. I think to better approach this, 
   Airbnb could cross-check directly with the city registration's database instead of having 
   hosts self report. This change would better keep in check the Engineering Code of Ethics
   as well as protecting the public good and maintaining integrity in software systems.

2. One important factor to consider in the legality of web scraping is whether the scraping
   causes harm or interferes with the websites functioning. Using Wikipedia, I found there are 
   several cases such as eBay v. Bidder's Edge as well as the QVC case, which focused not just 
   on access, but on whether the scraping activity overloaded servers or interrupted business 
   operations. In those situations, we found that QVC argued that excessive automated requests
   caused its website to crash and resulted in lost sales. This shows that legality is not just 
   about public data, but also about the impact of the scraping behavior. I think this factor 
   is important because consdering system harm helps balance innovation and accountability with
   fairness to the website owner and other users.

3. I think two important guidelines to consider before engaging in web scraping are: 1. Respect
   meaningful consent and the context in which the data was shared and 2. Minimize harm and ensure
   proportionality of usage. Regarding meaningful consent and the context in which we share data, we
   must understand that just because information is publicly available does not mean people consented
   to it being collected, aggregated, and repurposed. The Clearview AI case shows how scraping billions
   of publicly posted photos can fundamentally change how data is used, turning casual social media
   posts into a permanent facial recognition database. Here context is very important! Someone uploading 
   a photo to connect with friends is very different from consenting to being included in a law enforcement
   surveillance tool. I think if the use of web scraping changes the scale, permanence, or purpose of the 
   data in a way that harms autonomy or privacy, it should be reconsidered. Along with that, another 
   guideline to be considered is minimizing harm and ensure proprotionality of use. When data is scraped 
   it should only be used for clearly defined, legitimate, purposes, and the benefits should outweigh 
   the risks. Clearview AI show how large-scale scraping can lead to wrongful arrests, bias concerns,
   as well as mass surveillance. Before scraping, organizations should evaluate potential downstream harms
   such as discriminiation, misidentification, and reputational damage. Data collection can be done in a
   way where principles where collect only what is necessary is used, and security safegaurds, transparency,
   and accountability is all present. Overall, web scraping should prioritize informed expectations, 
   proportional usage, as well as harm reduction.
